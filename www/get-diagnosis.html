GETTING A DIAGNOSIS

STEP 1: REFERRAL TO IBD TEAM

If your GP suspects you may have Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) You should be referred on to a gastroenterology service


STEP 2: MEETING THE IBD TEAM: WHAT TO EXPECT IN THE CLINIC

The doctor will ask you some questions about your symptoms, what medications you take and your family medical history. You may have a brief physical exam based on what symptoms you have (ie pain in your abdomen)


STEP 3: BLOODS AND STOOL TESTS

Regardless of whether you have a suspected diagnosis of Ulcerative colitis or Crohnâ€™s disease most of the work up of tests are the same
You will need to give Blood and Stool tests* looking for signs of inflammation in your bowel. These tests will also help rule out other conditions such as infection or irritable bowel that may present with similar signs and symptoms to IBD.


STEP 4: ENDOSCOPY

Endoscopy is a camera test looking inside the bowel using an endoscope (which is a flexible tube with a light and a camera which transfers images from your bowel to a television screen). Your doctor can also use this instrument to take biopsies (tissue samples) from your bowel wall if needed.
There are two main types of tests, one limited to looking at the left side of your bowel (Sigmoidoscopy/Left Colonoscopy) and a longer scope looking at the entire large bowel (Full Colonoscopy).
It is important that the bowel is empty for the test, so that your doctor can get good clear views of views of the lining of the bowel wall.
For the full colonoscopy you will need to take a drink called bowel prep the night before the test to ensure your bowel is fully emptied.
For a sigmoidoscopy you do not need to take bowel prep but will be given an enema in the GI department on the day of the procedure.
You will be given sedation and pain relief to relax you throughout the procedure. As a result you will need someone to collect you after.
Both procedures are day cases, so you will be able to go home on the same day.

The doctor or nurse will give you preliminary results of the tests before you are discharged. If biopsies are taken, the results of these will be given at your next out-patient appointment.


STEP 5: XRAY/CT/US/MRI

Abdominal X-ray

This is an X-ray of your abdomen, which can be useful to check for inflammation and dilatation (widening) of your bowel.

CT Scan

Computed Tomography scans (or CT scans) are pictures taken by a specialised X-ray machine. The machine circles your body like a doughnut, and scans an area from every angle within that circle. It then relays that information to a computer, which generates a collection of black-and-white pictures, each showing a slightly different 'slice' or cross-section of your internal organs. This scan is more detailed than standard X-ray. You will have to fast prior to the scan and you may have to drink a special solution called contrast, which shows up on the scan and can help outline certain internal organs.

MRI

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (or MRI) is similar to CT but does not use X-rays. MRI uses magnets to send radio waves through the part of the body being examined, taking cross-sectional pictures. A computer puts the pictures together forming images of internal organs. In IBD, MRI is especially useful in looking for inflammation of the small intestine and fistulae, both internal and external of the pelvis and at the small bowel. You may be asked to drink a special drink prior to the scan depending on what area of your gastrointestinal tract your doctor wants examined. The MRI scanner can be noisy, and looks like a tunnel.
In the case of acute attacks or repeated flares, your doctor may decide to repeat any of the above tests or examinations to re-evaluate your disease and to help decide on further treatment strategies.


STEP 6: DIAGNOSIS

Your doctor will likely diagnose inflammatory bowel disease only after ruling out other possible causes for your signs and symptoms, including ischemic colitis, infection, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), diverticulitis and colon cancer. He or she will use a combination of tests.

TREATMENTS

Treatment of Crohnâ€™s disease can be divided into non-medical, medical and surgical. These treatments depend on the stage and severity of the disease.
Often a combination of all three may be necessary whether controlling active flares or maintaining stable disease.
The aim of these treatments is to heal the inflammation that is causing the above symptoms and help return to a normal quality life, stabilising the disease in the long term. The course of the disease is often unpredictable so treatments can change from time to time where necessary.
